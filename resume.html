Detailed Calculations - Trial 2

(1/2 cup vinegar + 1/2 teaspoon baking soda)

$$CH_{3}COOH_{(aq)} + {NaHCO_{3}}_{(s)} \rightarrow Na(CH_{3}COO)_{(aq)} + H_{2}O_{(l)} + {CO_{2}}_{(g)}$$

1. Calculate the volume of acetic acid used based on the 5% v/v ratio discussed on page 1.

First, we find volume of the vinegar using the density and mass given.

$$mass_{vinegar} = density_{vinegar} \cdot volume_{vinegar}$$

We know that the mass of the vinegar is 106.4g. We also know that the density of the vinegar is 1.05g/mL. Thus:

$$106.4g=\frac{1.05g}{1mL} \cdot volume_{vinegar}$$

$$volume_{vingear}=106.4g \cdot \frac{1mL}{1.05g}$$

$$volume_{vingear}=106.4\cancel{g} \cdot \frac{1mL}{1.05\cancel{g}}$$

$$volume_{vingear}=101.33$$

Now that we have the volume of the vinegar, we can find the volume of the acetic acid with the v/v ratio provided, which is 5% v/v.

$$v/v \: percent = \frac{volume_{acetic \: acid}}{volume_{vinegar}}$$

$$0.0500 = \frac{volume_{acetic \: acid}}{101.33mL}$$

$$volume_{acetic \: acid}=0.0500*101.33mL$$

$$volume_{acetic \: acid}=5.07mL$$

Therefore, the volume of the acetic acid is 5.07mL.

2. Using the density provided, calculate the mass of acetic acid used.

Using the density of vinegar as a replacement for acetic acid, along with the volume of the acetic acid that was previosly obtained, to find the mass of the acetic acid:

$$density = \frac{mass_{acetic \: acid}}{volume_{acetic \: acid}}$$

$$\frac{1.05g}{1mL} = \frac{mass_{acetic \: acid}}{5.07}$$

$$mass_{acetic \: acid}=\frac{1.05g}{1mL} \cdot 5.07mL$$

$$mass_{acetic \: acid}=\frac{1.05g}{1\cancel{mL}} \cdot 5.07\cancel{mL}$$

$$mass_{acetic \: acid}=5.32g$$

Therefore, the mass of the acetic acid is 5.32 grams.

3. Calculate the moles acetic acid used.

We can calculate the moles of acetic acid with the mass and molar mass of acetic acid.

We already have the mass of acetic acid. So we will find the molar mass of acetic acid:

$$Molar \: Mass_{CH_{3}COOH} = (12.01 \cdot 2) + (16.00 \cdot 2) + (1.01 \cdot 4)$$

$$Molar \: Mass_{CH_{3}COOH} = 60.06 \: g/mol$$

Now that we have the molar mass, we can find the number of moles:

$$n=\frac{m}{MM}$$

$$n=\frac{5.32g}{\frac{60.06g}{1mol}}$$

$$n=5.32g \cdot \frac{1mol}{60.06g}$$

$$n=5.32\cancel{g} \cdot \frac{1mol}{60.06\cancel{g}}$$

$$n=0.0886 \: mol$$

Therefore, we have used 0.0886 moles of acetic acid.

4. Calculate the moles of sodium bicarbonate used.

We can calculate the moles of sodium bicarbonate with the mass and molar mass of sodium bicarbonate.

We are given the mass of 1/2 teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate (2.79g), so we just need to find the molar mass of sodium bicardonate.$

$$Molar \: Mass_{NaHCO_{3}} = (22.99 \cdot 1) + (1.01 \cdot 1) + (12.01 \cdot 1) + (16.99 \cdot 3)$$

$$Molar \: Mass_{NaHCO_{3}} = 84.01 \: g/mol$$

Now that we have the molar mass, we can find the number of moles:

$$n=\frac{m}{MM}$$

$$n=\frac{2.79g}{\frac{84.01g}{1mol}}$$

$$n=2.79g \cdot \frac{1mol}{84.01g}$$

$$n=2.79\cancel{g} \cdot \frac{1mol}{84.01 \cancel{g}}$$

$$n= 0.0332 \: mol$$

Therefore, we have used 0.0332 moles of sodium bicarbonate.

5. Determine the limiting reagent.

We can determine the limiting reagent by comparing the numbers of moles of carbon dioxide produced by acetic acid and sodium bicarbonate.

Produced by acetic acid:

$$=0.0886 \: mol \: CH_{3}COOH \cdot \frac{1 \: mol \: CO_{2}}{1 \: mol \: CH_{3}COOH}$$

$$=0.0886 \: \cancel{mol \: CH_{3}COOH} \cdot \frac{1 \: mol \: CO_{2}}{1 \: \cancel{mol \: CH_{3}COOH}}$$

$$=0.0886 \: mol \: CO_{2}$$

Produced by sodium bicarbonate:

$$=0.0332 \: mol \: NaHCO_{3} \cdot \frac{1 \: mol \: CO_{2}}{1 \: mol \: NaHCO_{3}}$$

$$=0.0332 \: \cancel{mol \: NaHCO_{3}} \cdot \frac{1 \: mol \: CO_{2}}{1 \: \cancel{mol \: NaHCO_{3}}}$$

$$=0.0332 \: mol \: CO_{2}$$

Since the amount of moles of carbon dioxide produced by sodium bicarbonate is less, sodium bicarbonate is the limiting reagent in this reaction.

6.Calculate the expected amount of CO2 produced for your reaction.

From question 5, we know that about 0.0332 moles of carbon dioxide will be produced.

We can also find the mass of the carbon dioxide that will be produced.

To begin by finding the molar mass of carbon dioxide:

$$Molar \: Mass_{CO_{2}} = (12.01 \cdot 1) + (16.00 \cdot 2)$$

$$Molar \: Mass_{CO_{2}} = 44.01 \: g/mol$$

Now we can find the mass of the carbon dioxide produced:

$$n=\frac{m}{MM}$$

$$0.0332 \: mol=\frac{mass_{CO_{2}}}{\frac{44.01g}{1mol}}$$

$$0.0332 \: mol=mass_{CO_{2}} \cdot \frac{1mol}{44.01g}$$

$$mass_{CO_{2}}= 0.0332 \: \cancel{mol} \cdot \frac{44.01g}{1 \cancel{mol}}$$

$$mass_{CO_{2}}=1.46 \: g$$

Therefore, about 1.46 grams or 0.0332 moles of carbon dioxide is produced.
